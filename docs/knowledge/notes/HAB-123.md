### 2026-07-18

**Scope: ticket-specific constraints beyond docs/CONSTRAINTS.md**

Constraints unique to this ticket, surfaced before surveying: (1) The skill runs inside the Claude Code harness — available tools are WebSearch (general web, US-only), WebFetch (fetches one URL and answers a prompt against it via a small fast model — itself a summarization hop, relevant to hallucination compounding), and Bash (so free academic REST APIs are reachable via `curl`). No authenticated or paywalled sources. (2) No human reviewer exists between skill output and use, so the output format must be self-verifying: every claim must carry a working URL fetched in-session, checkable by the solo dev in minutes. (3) Token budget is a real bound — Anthropic reports multi-agent research systems use ~15x the tokens of a chat interaction (https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/multi-agent-research-system), so branch count and rounds must stay small. (4) Output should land as a markdown note, matching the existing `docs/knowledge/` conventions. The ticket's MoSCoW breakdown was adopted as-is; the only refinement is folding "checkable citations" into the Must tier as a hard output requirement rather than a Should — without it the no-reviewer constraint is violated.

**Alternatives survey: real precedent for all seven candidates**

1. *Dialectical (thesis → antithesis → synthesis).* Direct precedent exists. Hegelion is an active MIT-licensed library (169 stars, v0.5.0 released March 2026, MCP integration for Claude) implementing exactly this three-call loop — commit to a position, attack it from scratch in a separate call, synthesize (https://github.com/Hmbown/Hegelion). Academic precedent: "Self-reflecting Large Language Models: A Hegelian Dialectical Approach" (https://arxiv.org/html/2501.14917v3). The broader multi-agent-debate literature (Du et al.) shows debate improves factuality and reasoning (survey context: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2601.04742), and Khan et al.'s ICML 2024 best paper found a judge choosing between two LLM debaters reaches 76% accuracy vs a 48% naive baseline (https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.06782). Caution: "The Deliberative Illusion" finds *multi-round* multi-agent deliberation erases up to 72% of issue-critical facts and homogenizes stances while staying anchored in base-model priors (https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.03032) — the risk sits in iterated rounds, not in the single-pass triad.
2. *Systematic literature review.* Human baseline: mean 67.3 weeks to complete and publish, from the PROSPERO registry (Borah et al., BMJ Open 2017, https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28242767/). Automation exists — Covidence/Rayyan for screening (Rayyan reaches 97–99% sensitivity at a threshold in diagnostic-accuracy reviews, https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC9161508/), Elicit claims PRISMA-grade screening of 5,000+ papers on paid tiers (https://www.inra.ai/blog/covidence-vs-rayyan-vs-elicit) — but all presuppose database-scale retrieval, deduplication, and protocol-led screening infrastructure this harness does not have.
3. *Scoping review.* Well-codified as the lighter alternative: PRISMA-ScR (Tricco et al. 2018, https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30178033/) exists precisely for mapping "extent, range, and nature" of evidence without effect-size synthesis or completeness claims; guidance on choosing scoping over systematic: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6245623/. A good methodological fit for topic-shaped inputs ("what do we know about X?"), weak for adjudicating a claim.
4. *Plain adversarial prompting.* Grounded in the classic "consider the opposite" debiasing literature (https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0361476X20300096); "Failing to Falsify" shows LLMs exhibit confirmation bias and that Think-in-Opposites-style interventions consistently reduce it (https://arxiv.org/html/2604.02485v1); LLM-generated multi-persona debates reduce reader confirmation bias on controversial issues (https://arxiv.org/html/2412.04629v3). This matters because sycophancy is documented across all major assistants — models preferentially agree with the user's stated position (Sharma et al., https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.13548) — so *some* forced-opposition step is evidence-backed, whatever the framing.
5. *Steelman search.* The steelman concept is established argumentation practice (https://umbrex.com/resources/tools-for-thinking/what-is-steelmanning/) and appears in LLM argumentation research (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2510.02339), but no dedicated "find the single best-evidenced source per side" tool was found — as a standalone methodology it is precedent-thin. Its value is as a *scope-bounding rule* inside another methodology.
6. *SWOT-style framing.* LLMs demonstrably generate structured SWOT analyses from source documents (RAG-based investment-climate SWOT: https://medium.com/@rikimatsumoto/byte-sized-series-generating-structured-swot-analysis-from-macro-assessments-with-llms-3111c2b423f2; survey of LLMs in strategic analysis contexts: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2502.11919), but all found precedent targets product/strategy decisions, not truth-evaluation of factual claims — confirming the category mismatch the ticket suspected.
7. *Expert consensus mapping.* Two precedent families. Product: Consensus.app's Consensus Meter classifies the top 20 relevant papers from a 200M-paper index into yes/no/possibly with quality indicators (recency, methods, journal score, citations) (https://consensus.app/home/blog/introducing-the-consensus-meter/, https://help.consensus.app/en/articles/10069920-the-consensus-meter). Research: LLM-simulated Delphi panels closely matched human expert consensus where evidence was substantial but struggled where evidence was thin (https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41092428/), with a published caution that LLM-Delphi consensus can be a "mirage" reflecting model priors rather than evidence (https://doi.org/10.1097/JS9.0000000000004069). Replicating the product approach requires index-scale retrieval and a relevance classifier; the simulation approach generates consensus from priors, not literature — both poor fits here.

**Must-answers: search tools, hallucination compounding, buildability**

*Search-tool availability:* WebSearch and WebFetch cover the general web. For academic sources, free keyless/low-friction REST APIs are callable via Bash curl: Semantic Scholar Graph API (no key, ~100 requests/5 min, ~200M papers, https://www.semanticscholar.org/product/api), OpenAlex (250M+ works, 100k requests/day in the polite pool, https://libguides.ucalgary.ca/c.php?g=732144&p=5260804), the arXiv API, and Crossref (overview: https://intuitionlabs.ai/articles/research-paper-apis-scientific-literature). Google Scholar has no official API. Paywalled full text stays out of reach — the skill must work from abstracts, arXiv/PMC open-access copies, and general-web sources, and should say so in its output. So academic *metadata and abstracts* are realistically available; exhaustive full-text retrieval is not.

*Hallucination-compounding risk:* This is the sharpest documented failure mode of research agents. Citation-hallucination rates of 11–57% are reported across commercially deployed models (https://arxiv.org/html/2604.03173v1); deep-research agents show statement hallucination (content deviating from the cited source) and citation hallucination (fabricated references), with measured rates from 3.5% (OpenAI Deep Research) to 13.3% (Gemini 2.5 Pro Deep Research) plus a 10.1% dead-URL rate even at the low end (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2508.15804, https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.06635). And multi-round agent deliberation loses up to 72% of issue-critical facts (https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.03032). Mitigations that follow: (a) at most one synthesis hop over per-branch evidence tables — never summarize summaries of summaries; (b) the synthesis step may only reference claims already present in the branch evidence tables, never introduce new ones; (c) every claim carries the URL it came from, and URLs must have been successfully fetched in-session; (d) no iterated debate rounds. Note (own reasoning, not precedent): WebFetch's answer-via-small-model design already inserts one summarization hop per source, so the budget above is effectively spent — quote extraction should be preferred over paraphrase at the WebFetch layer.

*Which candidates are buildable and give "hear both sides" safely:* Dialectical and plain adversarial are both buildable single-session skills with strong evidence behind the forced-opposition step (Khan et al. https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.06782; sycophancy motivation https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.13548). Steelman and scoping are buildable but partial (bounding rule and topic-mapping mode respectively). Systematic review and consensus mapping are not realistically buildable to their own standards in this harness; SWOT is buildable but answers the wrong kind of question.

**Should/Could-answers: scope bounding, quality signal, parallelism**

*Scope bounding:* Adopt the steelman cap — 3–5 best-evidenced sources per branch, hard stop. Precedent for hard caps: the Consensus Meter classifies only the first 20 results (https://help.consensus.app/en/articles/10069920-the-consensus-meter). Search strategy should be broad-then-narrow, which Anthropic found necessary to stop agents issuing overlong specific queries that return nothing (https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/multi-agent-research-system). Date range and domain filters as optional skill arguments; WebSearch supports domain allow/block lists natively.

*Source-quality signal without a peer-review filter:* Semantic Scholar/OpenAlex metadata provides citation counts, venue, and publication year for free at search time (https://www.semanticscholar.org/product/api, https://intuitionlabs.ai/articles/research-paper-apis-scientific-literature). Consensus.app's four quality indicators — recency, study-design methods, journal quality, citations (https://help.consensus.app/en/articles/10069920-the-consensus-meter) — are a proven shape for a lightweight per-source quality line. For non-academic web sources, source-type labelling (peer-reviewed / preprint / vendor blog / forum) is own-reasoning heuristic, not precedent, and should be presented as such in skill output.

*Parallelism:* Feasible and proven at exactly this branch count. Anthropic's research system runs parallel search subagents and outperformed single-agent by 90.2% on research evals, at ~15x chat token cost for full multi-agent setups (https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/multi-agent-research-system). Two branches (thesis/antithesis) via the Agent tool is the cheap end of that curve and well within a solo dev's budget; anything beyond two parallel branches or one round is not justified by the evidence and is where the deliberative-illusion failure mode lives (https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.03032).

**Trade-off ranking against docs/CONSTRAINTS.md**

Ranked by fit to solo-dev / no-reviewer / pre-launch constraints, with the failure mode each risks: 1. *Dialectical (single-pass)* — best evidence for the judge-accuracy benefit; risk: synthesis step drifting from branch evidence (mitigated by evidence-table-only rule). 2. *Plain adversarial* — same benefit, one less step; risk: without a synthesis contract the output is two unreconciled lists the solo dev must reconcile manually, which is the reviewer-shaped work the constraints say does not exist. 3. *Steelman search* — cheapest, self-bounding; risk: single-source-per-side cherry-picking presents one paper as "the" evidence; best used as the bounding rule inside #1. 4. *Scoping review* — right tool for topic-mapping inputs, light; risk: gives no verdict on claims, so it fails the ticket's core "synthesized position" goal as a standalone. 5. *Expert consensus mapping* — needs index-scale retrieval to be honest; the LLM-simulated variant fabricates consensus from priors (https://doi.org/10.1097/JS9.0000000000004069); risk: confident consensus claims a solo dev cannot audit. 6. *Systematic review* — 67-week human baseline (https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28242767/) and PRISMA's completeness claim cannot be met with WebSearch + free APIs; risk: a report that *looks* PRISMA-grade but silently is not — the worst outcome under a no-reviewer constraint. 7. *SWOT* — category mismatch: precedent is strategy analysis, not claim evaluation; risk: forces evidence into quadrants that carry no truth semantics.

**Verdict**

Build one skill: single-pass dialectical with steelman-bounded evidence. Concretely: (1) restate the user's claim as a thesis and generate the strongest antithesis (evidence-backed forced opposition — Khan et al. https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.06782; motivated by documented sycophancy https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.13548); (2) research each branch in parallel via two subagents using WebSearch/WebFetch plus Semantic Scholar/OpenAlex curl calls, each returning an evidence table capped at 3–5 best sources with URL, quote, and quality line (citations/venue/year); (3) one synthesis pass that may only cite rows from those tables and must mark where each side holds, where the claim is contested, and what evidence was unavailable (paywalls, thin literature). No iterated debate rounds — that is where fact attrition and stance homogenization set in (https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.03032). For topic-shaped inputs with no claim to oppose, the skill should degrade to a scoping-style map (PRISMA-ScR spirit, https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30178033/) rather than manufacture a fake thesis. Rejected: systematic review (unmeetable standard in this harness), consensus mapping (unauditable or prior-driven), SWOT (wrong question type), plain adversarial as standalone (pushes reconciliation onto the missing human reviewer). "Build nothing" was considered and rejected: status-quo ad-hoc searching has no forced-opposition step, and the sycophancy evidence (https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.13548) says that is precisely the failure mode a claim-checking skill exists to prevent. This is reversible and pre-launch-appropriate: one skill file, no infrastructure, deletable in one commit.

Decision recorded as `docs/knowledge/decisions/ADR-0004-dialectical-research-skill-methodology.md`. Implementation tracked separately as HAB-178.
